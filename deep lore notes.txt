# The Veterans of Aerus: Conscription, Trauma, and the Rebellion

## The Estaran Philosophy of Civilization

The Estarans do not consider themselves conquerors. In their own understanding, they are stewards. They regard mortals as limited, lesser beings — children cursed to chaotic, short, and violent lives — and believe it is their duty to impose order on that chaos through civilization. They build cities, spread language, establish law. Every world they touch is, in their view, improved by their presence.

This is not cynical propaganda for external consumption. It is their genuine self-image. The Estarans are capable of tremendous cruelty not because they enjoy it but because their ordered, rule-bound nature narrows moral consideration until atrocities feel like administration. When they rebuild their empire after a reset of the cosmos, they do it the same way they always have: by civilizing mortals across worlds, world by world, generation by generation.

The Fey are the structural enemy of this project. Fey existence is fundamentally incompatible with Estaran magic and ideology. Every world with an Estaran footprint sees conflict with the Fey — not as a contingent political accident but as an expression of what both are. The Estarans know this. They have fought this war fifty-four times.

## The Conscription of Veterans

The Estarans understand something that mortals consistently fail to appreciate: mortals and Fey form bonds easily, and those bonds make mortals unwilling to fight Fey on their own world. A mortal soldier who has grown up alongside Fey, traded with Fey, loved a Fey — that soldier will not raise a sword against them when ordered to.

So the Estarans do not ask them to. Instead, they conscript the strongest and most capable mortal fighters from a given world and deploy them across worlds — to fight Fey the conscripts have never met, in places they cannot name, for reasons they are never given. To prevent the knowledge of other worlds from seeping back and threatening the Estarans' isolation strategy, the memories of service are crushed. Veterans return home knowing they were gone. They do not know where, or for how long, or what they did.

Sometimes the Estarans are able to conscript powerful Fey who have bonded closely to mortals — turning them against their own kind on distant worlds, severing or weaponizing the very bonds they formed. The mechanism is the bond itself: a Fey closely tied to mortal allies can be brought into the Estaran fold through those allies, whether or not the mortal intended any such consequence. Erol the Faed was one such case, drawn in through her deep friendship with Aldrin Stoneward. She does not blame him for this. But she cannot fully separate him from what followed, and that association is a persistent strain between them.

The psychic mechanisms used to suppress veterans' memories are an ancient Estaran tactic, refined across many iterations of the cosmos. They are not merely precautionary — they are a deliberate system of control, designed to mitigate the danger of rebellion by ensuring that veterans cannot piece together what was done to them. The traps are passive until probed, at which point they activate to prevent recovery. Veterans who attempt to explore their own past — through meditation, magic, or psychic introspection — encounter them without warning. This is almost certainly what Maelric encountered in his trance.

## Aerus: The Second Fey War and Its Conscripts

On Aerus, Estaran conscription of mortals and allied Fey began shortly before the Second Fey War, as conflicts on other worlds were becoming severe and the Estarans needed experienced fighters they could trust to follow orders without local loyalties complicating matters.

Among those conscripted were some of the most capable fighters Aerus had produced — including figures who would later become the founders of the Veterans' Rebellion and the Allied Cities. Erol the Faed was taken as well, brought into the Estaran fold through her bond with Aldrin.

The conflict on Ikellor is a parallel history, older than Aerus's Second Fey War. Ikellor is as old as Aerus, and the Estaran-Fey conflict there had been running for far longer — centuries, at minimum. The destruction of Ikellor's Feywild was not the beginning of that war but its terrible end: a deliberate strategic act by the Estarans to break Fey resistance permanently on that world. The result was an ice age that has been slowly killing the world ever since. Ikellor is very nearly dead when the party arrives. What the Aerus veterans were deployed to fight, and whether any of them served on Ikellor, is not recorded.

## What the Veterans Carry

The veterans of Aerus returned home damaged in ways they could not articulate and cannot explain. What they carry includes:

**A deep, persistent, sourceless fear.** They have seen things — committed things — they cannot recall. The fear remains even when the memory is gone. It surfaces without warning, disproportionate to present circumstances, resistant to reason.

**The scars of Fey psychic magic.** Many veterans were direct targets of Fey psychic attacks during their service. Fey psychic magic leaves particular kinds of wounds — not the blunt trauma of a suppressed memory but something more like a presence, a residue that lingers in the mind and does not heal cleanly.

**Unnatural longevity.** Estaran service altered the veterans physically. The Estarans frame this as a gift — the reward of a life well-spent in service to civilization. Mortals come to experience it as a curse. It is not a prize deliberately bestowed but the residue of what Estaran magic does to mortal bodies over extended exposure and service. Veterans do not naturally age out. On Aerus today, there are hundreds of them still alive in Aris — still present, still politically active, more than 150 years after the Rebellion ended — simply because they do not die. This is the engine of stagnation. The Young Bastards are not wrong that the Veterans have dominated political life for a century and a half. The Veterans are not wrong that they remember what was fought for. Neither can resolve this without something changing that isn't in anyone's power to change.

**An inability to grieve what they lost.** They know, in some sense, that time passed. They know they are different. They do not know what they lost, and they cannot mourn it cleanly. This is its own kind of wound.

## The Veterans' Rebellion

The Veterans' Rebellion on Aerus was not started by people who understood what had been done to them. It began almost intuitively — a breaking point among populations who had endured Estaran conscription, expenditure in foreign wars they did not remember, and the grinding toll of a conflict being fought on their home soil.

What the veterans perceived, without being able to name it clearly, was the evil at the core of the Estarans' purported benevolence. The civilization the Estarans had built was real. The roads, the cities, the language — all real. And underneath all of it was a system that conscripted their strongest, crushed their memories, used them as instruments against beings they might otherwise have called kin, and returned them broken without acknowledgment or apology.

The Rebellion eventually won, but not through military force alone. Milicent's Shadow War — fought in secret by wraiths and death knights across Aerus over decades — was the other half of the victory. It was not a war of great battles. It was a persistent campaign to collapse Estaran power at key structural points: supply lines, command nodes, communication infrastructure. This made the Estarans vulnerable enough that the Fey and the Veterans could deliver the final blow together. Most mortals on Aerus know that the Rebellion happened and that it succeeded. The role of Milicent's Dread Legion is completely unknown to them. The Fey themselves know it occurred but few know its full scope. The party, having spoken directly with Xilo and Milicent, now understands more than almost any living mortal.

## The Trauma That Persists

The Rebellion ended. The Estarans were driven from Aerus. The veterans did not heal.

The unresolved fear, the sourceless grief, the longevity that kept them alive and politically relevant — all of it persisted and hardened. In Aris, it became political dominance: not through conspiracy or malice but through the simple arithmetic of numbers and time. Hundreds of veterans who do not age, who remember fighting, who carry unhealed fear — of course they grip the available levers of safety. Of course the generations born after grow up in their shadow.

Maelric Sturmwald understood this better than most. He entered his trance deliberately, seeking a cure for the veterans' condition — and Gallifax, who kept vigil over him, understood why he had gone in. The party learned this after restoring him. What Maelric encountered in the trance, and whether he found anything useful before being trapped, is not yet known. It is likely he encountered the Estaran psychic trap.

The Erol and Babas theory from the Chapter 7 waterfront event — that the veterans' affliction may be deliberate Estaran tampering, a mechanism to prevent effective resistance even after conscription ends — is consistent with everything above. It is the in-world articulation of a truth the players now hold, even if most characters do not.

## Notes on Party Knowledge

The following summarizes what the party currently knows and doesn't know, as a guide for play:

**Known to the party:** The broad history of the Veterans' Rebellion. That veterans carry psychic damage of some kind. That the Estarans conscripted fighters from Aerus. That Maelric went into his trance seeking a cure. The Erol/Babas theory about deliberate Estaran tampering. The general shape of Milicent's Shadow War (that it occurred, that it was a secret campaign, that it helped the Rebellion win).

**Not known to the party:** The full details of Erol's personal timeline — her bond with Aldrin as the mechanism of her conscription, the strain this created, her path from Milicent to Xilo to the Teeth. The scope of Ikellor's history. The precise nature of the psychic traps or how they might be removed. Whether Maelric found anything useful before being trapped in his trance.

**In tension / unresolved:** Whether the veterans' longevity can be undone, and whether any of them would choose that if it could. What Maelric actually encountered. Whether the Estaran tampering can be specifically identified and targeted as a future story thread.
